DNA Replication Timing and Stem Cell Potential of the Intestinal Epithelium

PhD Thesis, Hinal Tanna

Abstract

Eukaryotic DNA replication occurs in a mitotically inherited temporal order known as the replication timing program. Replication timing is regulated by the coordinated simultaneous firing of adjacent replication origins at the level of large chromosomal units called ‘replication domains’. The replication timing program is cell-type specific and in a close inter-dependent relationship with the sub-nuclear positioning of the DNA, the epigenetic landscape of the chromatin, and the gene expression profile.

Stem and progenitor cell populations could be used to treat and regenerate tissues. To make this possible, a huge research effort is focused on identifying and characterizing the behavior and differentiation potential of stem cells. In this thesis, I hypothesize that the replication timing program of cell populations can be used to infer stem cell potential by giving a read-out of the transcriptional availability of the genome. The intestinal epithelium of the mouse is the stem cell supported tissue in which this hypothesis is explored.

Protocols for the isolation of intestinal epithelial cells at G1- and S-phase stages of the cell cycle were applied and DNA libraries suitable for next generation (Illumina) whole genome sequencing were prepared. This allowed replication timing profiles of embryonic stem cells, spheroid cultures of foetal small intestine (Foetal Enterospheres), and the in vivo adult epithelium of both the small intestine and colon to be produced.

Whole genome replication timing profiles are produced from DNA copy-number differences between cells at different stages of cell cycle. The biggest bottleneck in producing high-quality replication timing profiles is obtaining a high signal-to-noise ratio. Therefore, I have developed and optimized computational methods for producing replication timing profiles based on DNA copy-number differences between G1- and S-phase cells, and for identifying replication timing differences between profiles.

The computational methods were used to study the relationship between replication timing and stem cell potential during embryogenesis of the mouse intestinal epithelium. This analysis showed that more genes switch replication timing in either early or late development, rather than undergoing a gradual replication timing change throughout development. Gene Ontology analysis of differentially replicating genes revealed that replication timing switches during development correlate with phenotypic changes. This thesis describes the first replication timing profile for an in vivo epithelial tissue and may provide a basis for predicting stem cell potential using replication timing profiles, eliminating the need for retrospective functional assays.
